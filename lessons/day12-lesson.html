What supplements actually are

Supplements supplement your current nutrition. That's it. Good, balanced meals and consistent exercise still remain your number one priority. Adding supplements doesn't mean you can ignore these foundations.

This is also not a doctor's recommendation. If you have any existing health conditions, please speak to your doctor before adding any supplement. And if you're ever unsure whether something is right for you, reach out to us - we can help you work through it.

What we typically recommend

Across this three-part series, we'll cover the supplements we commonly suggest at GetFit AF:

  • Whey protein (or any protein powder)
  • Omega-3 / fish oil
  • Vitamin D3
  • A good multivitamin
  • Creatine

Whey protein

Whey protein is not harmful. It's a very safe supplement - and one of the most useful, because a lot of people, especially in Indian households, are nowhere near the recommended daily protein intake.

The target I usually recommend: 1.8 to 2.2g of protein per kg of body weight per day.

Most of us are getting less than 0.8g per kg. To put that in context - 0.8g per kg is the minimum needed just to avoid malnutrition. Most of us aren't even meeting that. So the gap is significant, and whey protein is one of the most practical ways to close it.

Types of whey protein

Whey protein comes in a few forms. Here's what to know:

  • Whey Protein Concentrate - the most common form; works well for most people
  • Whey Protein Isolate - lower in lactose; a better choice if you feel bloated or uncomfortable on concentrate
  • Whey Protein Hydrolysate - pre-digested and easiest on the stomach; good option if isolate still causes issues

If none of these sit well with you, a plant-based protein powder is a perfectly good alternative. If you're not sure which option to go with, reach out to your coach and we'll help you decide.

Always look for third-party certifications when buying any protein product - it ensures you're getting what the label says.

A note on protein bars

Protein bars are not the same as protein powder. A lot of them are glorified chocolates dressed up in fitness packaging.

The rule to follow: look for at least a 1:10 protein-to-calorie ratio. If a bar has 20g of protein, the maximum it should have is around 200 calories. Many bars give you only 10g of protein for close to 250 calories - that's well below 1:10, which means they're not really a protein source worth counting on.

That being said, swapping a regular chocolate for a protein bar once in a while is still a reasonable choice - just make sure the protein to calorie ratio is there.

When to take whey protein

There's a common misconception that you need to rush and drink a shake immediately after your workout, or the benefits disappear. That's not true. You can have whey protein at any point throughout the day.

One situation where timing does matter: If you're training on a completely empty stomach, there are no free-flowing amino acids available for your muscles to use. Having a scoop of protein before that workout can give a small boost to the muscle-building stimulus. Even so, the effect is minor - don't stress about it.

Another situation: if you already have a good protein source in your meal in sufficient quantities, it's a good idea to space your protein powder out and have it at least one to two hours later.

That said, the most important thing is simply hitting your daily protein target. Total intake matters far more than exact timing.

Today's takeaway: Supplements work on top of good nutrition and exercise - not instead of them. If your protein intake is consistently low, whey protein is one of the most practical ways to bridge that gap.
