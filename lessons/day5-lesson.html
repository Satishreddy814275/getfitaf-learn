Lesson 5 - Part 2

Protein sources, how much to eat, and what to look for

Yesterday I walked you through why protein matters. Today let's get into the practical side - what to eat, how much, and what to actually look for on a label.

What counts as a protein source?

Most foods are a combination of all three macronutrients - protein, carbs, and fats. We label something as a protein source when it has more protein than fats or carbs.

You'll see a lot of foods marketed as "high protein" - but not all of them actually are. Flip the packet over, or Google the food, and check the breakdown. If it has more carbs and fats than protein, it's not really a protein source regardless of what the packaging says.

Here are some great protein sources:

  • Meat - chicken, fish, mutton, and similar
  • Eggs
  • Paneer (one of the best options if you're vegetarian)
  • Beans - chickpeas, kidney beans, black-eyed peas
  • Some lentils - moong dal is a great example
  • Soya chunks (also called meal maker)
  • Tofu
  • Tempeh
  • Greek yogurt
  • Protein supplements - whey or plant-based

This isn't an exhaustive list. There are plenty of other options out there.

Lean protein - why it matters on a fat loss journey

When you're trying to lose fat, choosing lean protein sources is going to serve you much better. A lean protein is simply a protein source that comes with fewer overall calories - meaning you get the protein without a lot of extra fat or carbs alongside it.

For non-vegetarians
  • Chicken breast
  • Egg whites
  • Lean fish - salmon, tuna, rohu, katla, tilapia
For vegetarians
  • Soya chunks
  • Tofu
  • Tempeh
  • Greek yogurt

Both can also use a protein supplement. If you're vegan, a plant-based option works well here.

A quick note on protein supplements

Whey protein is not a steroid. It is not harmful. It is not "unnatural."

Many people avoid whey protein because it is "unnatural." But they go ahead and have all the chocolates in the world 😂

Two situations where you'd want to be careful:

  • If you don't tolerate dairy well, or are lactose intolerant - go for a plant-based protein powder instead
  • If you have any kidney issues - check with your doctor first

One more thing: if you are relying too much on protein powder, you will miss out on the vitamins and minerals that come with whole food protein sources. Use it as a supplement to a varied diet, not a replacement for one.

Which brand should you choose?

Look for something that is third-party tested. This means an independent body has verified that what's on the label is actually what's in the product. Labdoor, ConsumerLab, and NSF are three places you can check.

How much protein should you be eating?

0.8g per kg of body weight is the bare minimum your body needs to avoid deficiency. That is not the ideal - it's just the floor. Unfortunately, most people I work with are eating well below even this number.

Ideally, you want to aim for 1.8 to 2.2g per kg of body weight - especially when you're in a fat loss phase. Beyond 2.2g, the additional benefit is minimal.

You don't need to make a dramatic switch and start with 1.8 to 2.2 immediately. Observe how much protein you are consuming and increase it by just 0.1g per kg. If you feel good, gradually increase it further until you reach 1.8g per kg of body weight.

How to measure it without counting every gram

If tracking grams feels like too much work, use your hand instead.

Think of the non-finger part of your palm - that surface area is one portion. Aim for 1 to 2 portions like that in every meal. Start with 1 if that already feels like a stretch, and build from there.

Quick visual guide

Place your hand flat, fingers forward. The area from the base of your fingers down to your wrist - that square of palm - is one portion. Not the fingers. Just the palm.

Today's takeaway: Focus on getting a palm-sized portion of protein in every meal. Choose lean sources where possible. And gradually work your way up towards 1.8g per kg of body weight - you don't have to get there overnight.
