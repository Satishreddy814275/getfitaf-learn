Lesson 9

How much weight should you be lifting?

One of the most common questions I get is: how much weight should I be lifting?

The reason we don't give you a specific number is that it's extremely challenging to estimate someone's strength levels. Whatever number I give you at this point will almost always be a little too much or a little too little for you.

So here's what I suggest instead.

Start with a weight that feels very, very easy.

In the beginning, the goal is to understand the form of the exercise - not to impress anyone with the weight. If you're completely new to exercise, that might be 2.5 kg or 5 kg. If you've done some form of exercise before, use your current baseline as a reference and start somewhere that feels comfortable.

The weight doesn't matter at the start. What matters is that you are gradually lifting heavier over time. Let me explain exactly how to do that.

The GetFit AF progressive overload system

Let's say you're doing the bench press, and I've given you 3 sets of 10 reps. You start with 10 kg and you're able to hit 10 reps for all three sets.

That means it's time to increase the weight in the next session.

Go to the next available increment in your gym - that might be 12.5 kg or 15 kg. When you pick up the new weight, three things can happen:

Scenario 1: You hit the target again.

You take the heavier weight and you're still able to do 10 reps for all 3 sets. Increase the weight again next session.

Scenario 2: You got more than half the reps.

You took the heavier weight and got 6 or 7 reps out of 10. Stick with the new weight for all three sets - even if you only get 6 or 7 reps per set. Your goal now is to slowly work your way up to 10 reps across all three sets. Once you hit that, increase the weight again.

Scenario 3: You got less than half the reps.

You took the heavier weight and only got 3 or 4 reps. In this case, do your first set with the new weight - then go back to your old weight for sets 2 and 3, hitting your full 10 reps as normal. (Set 1: new weight → Sets 2 & 3: old weight, full reps.)

Over time, build your reps with the new weight: 3 → 4 → 5 → 6 → 7. Once you cross the halfway mark, switch the new weight to all three sets. Work it up to 10 × 3 - and repeat the whole process.

One important note on safety.

If you ever feel unusual pain during an exercise, stop. Don't push through it.

If you're having a low energy day - bad sleep, high stress, just not feeling it - you don't need to force a weight increase. There will be plenty of high-energy days. On those days, push yourself. On the harder days, just show up and do the work at whatever level feels right.

The weight will definitely go up. You just have to keep showing up.

Today's takeaway: Start easy, focus on form, and increase the weight whenever you're hitting your target reps. Use the three-scenario system to decide when to push up and when to hold steady.
